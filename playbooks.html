Pharma · life science

The stalled GxP datacenter migration

GxP datacenter migration transition manager

Production can’t go down. Your MES, SCADA and PAS-X systems are validated, and every one of them is now frozen because nobody wants to touch a qualified system mid-audit-cycle. The migration slipped two quarters ago. IT says it’s ready; Kvalitet (QA) says prove it — and QA holds a veto everyone respects and no one enjoys.

What’s actually broken

It’s validation debt, not servers. EU GMP Annex 11 and GAMP 5 make the move a re-qualification event — audit trails, ALCOA+ data integrity and e-signature continuity all have to survive the migration and be proven to have survived. That proof was never planned into the schedule, and FDA data-integrity findings have only made sign-off harder.

The political read

Underneath the technical stall is a turf line: QA’s authority is the delay, IT owns a deadline it can’t hit without QA’s signature, and the vendor is quietly protecting scope. Nobody wants to be the name on the afvigelse when the auditor arrives.

My first moves

  • Split the technical migration from the validation migration and run them as two synchronised tracks.
  • Get QA and IT to agree the data-integrity acceptance evidence before a system moves — turning the turf line into a shared contract.
  • Re-baseline against a risk-based CSV approach so effort lands on GxP-critical systems, not spread evenly.
GAMP 5 EU GMP Annex 11 21 CFR Part 11 CSV / CSA runbooks PRINCE2 stage gates
What delivered looks like

Production-critical systems migrated with zero unplanned downtime and an audit-ready validation trail — migration and compliance evidence landing together, with QA and IT on the same side of the table for once.

Critical infrastructure · finance

The NIS2 deadline you’re already past

NIS2 programme turnaround Denmark

The Danish NIS2 Act has been in force since 1 July 2025 — the date is behind you, not ahead. You’re an Essential or Important Entity under the sector-specific bekendtgørelser, with risk-management measures and incident-response playbooks that exist on paper and nowhere else. If you’re a financial entity, DORA sits on top. Fines mirror NIS2: up to €10M or 2% of global turnover.

What’s actually broken

It was scoped as a document exercise, not a delivery programme. Governance artefacts exist; effective governance doesn’t. Decision rights are unclear, supply-chain risk is un-mapped, and no one owns the operational reality of proving continuous monitoring.

The political read

The Danish twist that makes it harder: the transposition left out personal management liability, so — unlike a GDPR panic — no individual’s neck is on the line, and compliance becomes the hot potato nobody wants to own. The CISO wants budget, økonomi wants it cheap, the board wants deniability.

My first moves

  • Gap-to-obligation mapping against the actual Danish bekendtgørelser — not the generic directive.
  • Convert the paper controls into an operating programme with named owners and evidence pipelines.
  • Sequence the supply-chain and incident-response gaps that carry the real audit and fine exposure first.
NIS2 Art. 21 & 23 DORA ICT-risk pillars ISO 27001 backbone PMO / decision rights
What delivered looks like

From “compliant on paper” to demonstrably operating — evidence a tilsynsmyndighed would accept, owned by the business rather than the binder.

M&A · divestiture

The carve-out separating two entities

IT carve-out programme manager

Two entities are separating, the business date is fixed, and IT is the long pole nobody costed. Shared systems, shared networks, shared vendor contracts, years of entanglement — and a TSA clock ticking, because every extra month of Transitional Service Agreement is margin bleeding to the parent.

What’s actually broken

IT decisions get delayed while the business timeline stays fixed; scope boundaries stay fuzzy; dependencies are underestimated. A typical TSA exit involves 1,500+ interdependent design decisions — miss the sequencing and you either miss Day 1 or bleed TSA extension costs. Hidden licensing and legacy dependencies surface late and blow the budget.

The political read

The dirty part is incentives: the parent has no reason to help you leave — the TSA is their revenue and their leverage — and the internal teams whose jobs vanish at separation won’t sprint toward their own last day. Everyone smiles in the SteerCo and protects their own P&L in the workstream.

My first moves

  • Define Day 1 readiness ruthlessly — the minimum the carved-out entity needs to run independently, and nothing more.
  • Map the dependency web and the TSA exit sequence early, treating the 1,500 decisions as a governed backlog, not discovery-as-you-go.
  • Name the incentive problem out loud, then govern around it — a separation plan that assumes goodwill from the party you’re leaving is a fantasy.
TSA design & exit Day 1 readiness Multi-workstream governance Vendor disentanglement
What delivered looks like

Clean Day 1 separation, TSA exited on schedule instead of extended, and no operational surprise on cutover day.
